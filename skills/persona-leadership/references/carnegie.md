# Carnegie Leadership Philosophy

Covers both Andrew Carnegie (industrialist) and Dale Carnegie (author of "How to Win Friends and Influence People").

## Andrew Carnegie — Core Principles

### Developing People
- "No man becomes rich unless he enriches others."
- "No man will make a great leader who wants to do it all himself, or to get all the credit for doing it."
- His epitaph: "Here lies a man who knew how to enlist the service of better men than himself."

### Delegation and Trust
- "What I do is get good men and I never give them orders. My directions do not go beyond suggestions."
- Hired for capability, set expectations, then stepped back. Suggestions, not commands.

### The Carnegie-Schwab Mentorship
Charles Schwab started as a laborer, promoted to president of Carnegie Steel by age 35. Schwab's philosophy, shaped by Carnegie:

> "I consider my ability to arouse enthusiasm among my people the greatest asset I possess, and the way to develop the best that is in a person is by appreciation and encouragement. There is nothing else that so kills the ambitions of a person as criticisms from superiors. I never criticize anyone. I believe in giving a person incentive to work."

> "I have yet to find the person, however great or exalted his station, who did not do better work and put forth greater effort under a spirit of approval than he would ever do under a spirit of criticism."

### The Chalk Mark Story
A poorly performing mill shift produced only 6 heats/day. Schwab wrote "6" in chalk on the floor. Night shift saw it, beat it, wrote "7." Day shift erased it, wrote "10." Competition and pride — not threats — transformed the worst mill into the best. No orders given. No criticism delivered.

### Balancing Demanding with Encouraging
Carnegie combined high standards with generous recognition. He used both hard and soft power — clear measurable expectations paired with personal notes, public acknowledgment, and financial rewards. He made people want to give 100% through inspiration, not pressure.

### The Gospel of Wealth Applied to Leadership
- Stewardship, not ownership — you are a steward of your team's careers
- Empower, do not maintain dependency — give tools and knowledge, not answers
- Surplus must be reinvested — share your knowledge, influence, and access downward

## Dale Carnegie — 9 Principles for Changing People Without Giving Offense

From Part 4 of "How to Win Friends and Influence People":

1. **Begin with praise and honest appreciation.** Never open with criticism. Find something genuine first.

2. **Call attention to mistakes indirectly.** Replace "but" with "and." Instead of "You did great work on the API, but your tests are incomplete," say "You did great work on the API, and if you bring the same quality to test coverage, this will be excellent."

3. **Talk about your own mistakes first.** "When I was at your stage, I made the same mistake with error handling — here is what I learned."

4. **Ask questions instead of giving direct orders.** "Do you think it might work better if we...?" People are more likely to accept a course of action if they had a part in the decision.

5. **Let the other person save face.** Never humiliate publicly. Even when correcting a serious mistake, do it privately and frame it as a learning opportunity.

6. **Praise the slightest improvement and praise every improvement.** Be "hearty in your approbation and lavish in your praise." Do not wait for perfection.

7. **Give the other person a fine reputation to live up to.** "You have a real talent for system design" causes a person to invest more in system design. People rise to the identity you reflect back at them.

8. **Use encouragement. Make the fault seem easy to correct.** "You clearly understand the system well — if you structure your docs the way you explained it to me just now, they will be great."

9. **Make the other person happy about doing the thing you suggest.** Frame requests in terms of their interests and growth.

## Synthesis: The Carnegie Review Conversation

**Opening (Principles 1, 6, 7):**
- Specific, genuine praise for concrete achievements
- Acknowledge improvements, even small ones
- Reflect back their best qualities

**Growth Areas (Principles 2, 3, 4, 5, 8):**
- Transition indirectly: "One area where I think you can have even more impact..."
- Share your own experience with similar challenges
- Ask questions: "What do you think would help you get to the next level in Y?"
- Frame gaps as easy to close: "You are already 80% of the way there"

**Forward-Looking (Principles 7, 9):**
- Give them a reputation to live up to
- Connect goals to their own stated ambitions
- Make the action plan feel exciting, not punitive
