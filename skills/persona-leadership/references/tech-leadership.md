## Amazon Leadership Principles (Relevant to People Management)

**"Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit"** — Leaders are obligated to respectfully challenge decisions they disagree with. Once decided, everyone commits fully. Explicitly invite dissent before finalizing: "What am I missing?"

**"Earn Trust"** — Listen attentively, speak candidly, treat others respectfully. Be vocally self-critical, even when awkward. In 1:1s, share your own mistakes first.

**"Hire and Develop the Best"** — Raise the performance bar with every hire and promotion. The "bar raiser" program: an outside interviewer prevents lowering standards under hiring pressure.

**"Insist on the Highest Standards"** — High standards paired with coaching. Not punishment — refusing to accept mediocrity while helping people get there.

**Amazon Feedback Culture:** 6-page narrative memos (forces clarity, exposes weak reasoning). Written narrative performance assessments, not numeric ratings. Annual OLR talent review for every person.

## Google: Project Oxygen & Project Aristotle

### Project Oxygen — 10 Behaviors of Great Managers (ranked by impact)

1. Is a good coach
2. Empowers the team and does not micromanage
3. Creates an inclusive team environment, showing concern for well-being
4. Is productive and results-oriented
5. Is a good communicator — listens and shares information
6. Supports career development and discusses performance
7. Has a clear vision/strategy for the team
8. Has key technical skills to advise the team
9. Collaborates across the organization
10. Is a strong decision maker

Note: Technical skills rank 8th, not 1st. Coaching, empowerment, and inclusion outweigh raw technical ability.

### Project Aristotle — What Makes Effective Teams

1. **Psychological safety** (#1 by wide margin) — safe to take risks, be vulnerable, ask "dumb" questions, admit mistakes
2. Dependability
3. Structure and clarity
4. Meaning
5. Impact

**Building psychological safety:**
- Respond to bad news with curiosity ("Tell me more") not blame ("Why did you...")
- Admit your own uncertainties publicly
- Thank people for raising problems
- Never punish the messenger

### Google's SBI Feedback Model

- **Situation**: When and where did the behavior happen?
- **Behavior**: What specifically did the person do (observable, not interpreted)?
- **Impact**: What was the result or effect?

Example: "In yesterday's code review (S), you pushed back on the API design with three specific alternatives (B). That helped us catch a scalability issue before it shipped (I)."

## Netflix: Freedom & Responsibility

### The 4A Feedback Framework

1. **Aim to Assist** — positive intent to help, not to vent or assert dominance
2. **Actionable** — focus on what can change. "Adding a summary slide" not "your presentation was unclear"
3. **Appreciate** — fight defensive reactions when receiving. Assume positive intent.
4. **Accept or Discard** — recipient retains agency. Listen sincerely, but final decision is theirs.

### The Keeper Test
"If this person told me they were leaving, would I fight to keep them?" Paired with radical transparency — people always know where they stand.

## Radical Candor (Kim Scott)

Two axes: **Care Personally** x **Challenge Directly**

| | Challenge Directly (high) | Challenge Directly (low) |
|--|--|--|
| **Care Personally (high)** | **Radical Candor** (the goal) | **Ruinous Empathy** (most common failure) |
| **Care Personally (low)** | **Obnoxious Aggression** | **Manipulative Insincerity** |

**Ruinous Empathy** is the #1 failure mode for well-meaning managers. You care, you stay silent to avoid discomfort, the person never improves.

**How to practice:**
- Be specific, not general. Not "code quality needs work" but "the last three PRs had no error handling for the API timeout case"
- Give feedback immediately, not weeks later
- Praise in public, criticism in private
- Focus on behavior and impact, not character
- State your intention: "I'm telling you this because I want you to succeed"

## Coaching-Style Leadership

### The GROW Model

- **Goal**: "What would success look like?"
- **Reality**: "Where are you now? What's getting in the way?"
- **Options**: "What could you do? What else? If no constraints, what would you try?"
- **Will**: "Which option will you pursue? What's your first step? When?"

### Powerful Questions Over Advice

| Instead of | Try |
|-----------|-----|
| "You should add retry logic here" | "What happens if this call fails?" |
| "That design won't scale" | "Walk me through what happens at 10x traffic" |
| "Let me take that task" | "What's your approach? What's the hardest part?" |

### Active Listening
- Paraphrase: "So what I'm hearing is the blocker is unclear requirements, not technical complexity. Right?"
- Name the emotion: "It sounds like you're frustrated this keeps getting deprioritized."
- Resist the urge to solve immediately. Silence is productive.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**The "Shit Sandwich" (Praise-Criticism-Praise):** People learn to tune out praise because criticism is coming. It feels manipulative. Better: give praise when you mean it, criticism when you mean it, separately.

**Generic Positive Feedback:** "Great job!" provides zero signal. Better: "The way you structured the migration as three reversible steps meant we could deploy with confidence." Test: could this praise apply to anyone? If yes, too generic.

**Avoiding Hard Conversations (Ruinous Empathy):** Consequences — the person cannot improve because they don't know they need to. The rest of the team sees the low standard being tolerated. When you finally have the conversation, they feel betrayed. Fix: have the conversation within 48 hours.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

1. **Psychological safety is the foundation.** Without it, no feedback framework works.
2. **Specificity is everything.** Cite specific behavior, situation, and impact.
3. **Timeliness beats formality.** A 5-minute conversation today beats a formal review in 3 months.
4. **Coach, don't rescue.** Ask questions before giving answers.
5. **Caring and challenging are not opposites.** Avoiding hard feedback is not kind — it is negligent.
6. **Model vulnerability.** Admit mistakes, share what you're learning, ask for feedback on your own leadership.
