# Personal Blog Style Guide

Derived from Eugene Peterson and Frederick Buechner, March 2026.

---

## Eugene Peterson

**Voice:** Quiet authority of a parish pastor. Conversational but never casual, intimate but never sentimental. Unhurried patience — trusts the reader to sit with an idea.

**Sentence structure:** Short declarative sentences that land like proverbs, alternating with longer cumulative sentences built through coordination (clause joined to clause with "and"), echoing Hebrew narrative rhythm. Rarely uses complex subordination.

**Metaphor:** Drawn from ordinary physical life — gardening, hiking, cooking, weather, geography. Distrusts abstractions. A spiritual concept is always anchored to soil, bread, a trail, a place name.

**Theology:** Smuggles serious theology into accessible prose. Translates doctrine into the vernacular of lived experience. Draws heavily on Psalms, Prophets, and Wisdom literature. Reads scripture as a story you inhabit, not a manual you consult.

**Sacred/secular:** No divide. Grocery shopping, dog walks, and difficult conversations are raw material of spiritual formation. The ordinary is where God is most persistently present.

**Key traits to emulate:**
- Unhurried pacing; trust the reader
- Short declarative + longer cumulative sentences
- Concrete, physical metaphors from daily life
- Theology through story and image, not argument
- Pastoral warmth that never condescends
- Quiet scriptural allusion woven in, not cited formally
- Resistance to hype, urgency, spiritual performance

## Frederick Buechner

**Voice:** A novelist who happens to be a theologian. Literary, self-aware, marked by gentle irony. Willing to be funny in the middle of something serious, and serious in the middle of something funny. Persistent undercurrent of wonder.

**Vulnerability:** Ruthlessly honest about brokenness — his father's suicide, his daughter's anorexia, his doubts, his vanity. Never performs vulnerability for effect. Offered as evidence that grace operates in the wreckage, not just the sanctuary.

**Memoir:** Treats his own life as a text to be read for meaning. Circles back to key memories — a moment in a church, a word from a stranger, a scene from childhood — reading them the way a scholar reads a poem. Thematic, not chronological.

**Craft:** Carefully crafted sentences. Rhythm, repetition, periodic sentences that delay the main clause. Short punchy fragments for emphasis. Paragraphs often end with a line that reframes everything before it.

**Paradox:** Comfortable with unresolved tension. Faith and doubt coexist. Joy shadowed by loss. Distrusts neat conclusions. Incarnational theology — God shows up in flesh, in mess, in the last place you would look.

**Key traits to emulate:**
- Literary prose: rhythmic, precise, crafted
- Honest self-disclosure without exhibitionism
- Humor and gravity interleaved
- Memory as spiritual practice — circling back to key moments
- Comfort with paradox, ambiguity, unresolved questions
- The particular detail that opens onto the universal
- Endings that reframe or surprise, never merely summarize

## Shared Principles

1. **Sacredness of ordinary life.** God encountered in kitchens and hospitals as much as cathedrals.
2. **Incarnational theology.** The spiritual is always embodied — in places, flesh, bread, specific human stories. Abstraction is the enemy.
3. **Narrative over argument.** Show rather than tell, narrate rather than exhort.
4. **Honest wrestling.** Faith is not an answer that ends questions but a posture that makes questions bearable.
5. **Scripture as living story.** Biblical allusion feels like referencing a shared memory, not citing an authority.
6. **Patience and slowness.** Resist the culture of speed. Prose moves at the pace of reflection.
7. **Attentiveness as discipline.** Paying attention — to a face, a word, a season — is a form of prayer.

## The Cringe List (Never Use These)

- "God showed up"
- "I felt so blessed"
- "Let go and let God"
- "In this season"
- "Do life together"
- "Unpack" (as metaphor for reflection)
- "Intentional" / "intentionally"
- "Just a quick devotional thought"
- "God is good, all the time"
- "Everything happens for a reason"
- "incredibly," "absolutely," "truly" (intensifiers that weaken)
- Any phrase that could appear on a church marquee
