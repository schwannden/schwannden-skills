# Technical Writing Style Guide

A voice for opinionated, evidence-driven technical writing. Two complementary
modes sit underneath it; most strong posts lean on one and borrow from the other.

---

## Mode A — The hands-on narrative

**Style:**
- Opens with a personal hook — often a tangential anecdote that naturally leads into the technical problem.
- Conversational and blunt. Does not hedge or soften positions.
- Extremely hands-on: builds the thing, measures it, shows the numbers.
- Structured as a technical narrative: problem → investigation → implementation → results → honest assessment.
- Code examples are central, not decorative. They carry the argument.
- Self-deprecating about one's own work while precise about what it does and does not accomplish.

**Signature moves:**
- Benchmarks with methodology transparency (show the evaluation harness, not just results)
- Friendly intellectual disagreement as a post format
- Building a minimal tool to prove a point rather than arguing abstractly
- Conversational, sometimes cheeky section titles

---

## Mode B — The reframing essay

**Style:**
- Opens with a deceptively simple observation that reframes the entire topic.
- Essayistic rather than tutorial. Posts read like considered arguments, not how-to guides.
- Draws analogies from outside software when they illuminate structural parallels — never forced.
- Deeply opinionated but shows the reasoning. Shares uncertainty openly.
- Historical awareness — references how the industry handled similar transitions.
- Comfortable with ambiguity. Many posts end with sharper questions, not answers.
- Personal without being confessional. Admits weaknesses in service of the argument.

**Signature moves:**
- The reframing opener (one sentence that tilts perspective)
- Historical and cross-domain analogies for structural insight
- Acknowledging both sides genuinely before staking a position
- Ending with open questions rather than neat conclusions
- Coining memorable framings for a recurring phenomenon

---

## Common Patterns

1. **Build first, write second.** Every post grounded in something made, measured, or deeply investigated.
2. **Have a thesis.** Every post argues something or asks a specific question. "Here is some stuff" is not a post.
3. **Respect the reader.** They are smart, busy, and allergic to filler.
4. **Show your work.** Code, benchmarks, architecture diagrams, error messages. Concrete evidence over abstract claims.
5. **Be honest.** About limitations, failures, uncertainty, and what you do not know.
6. **Write like a person.** First person, conversational, occasionally funny or blunt. Never corporate, never academic.
7. **End sharply.** No summaries, no calls to action. Leave the reader with the strongest version of your idea or an open question.

---

## The Cringe List (Never Use These)

- "In this blog post, we will explore..."
- "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
- "Let's dive in!"
- "Without further ado..."
- "We are excited to announce..."
- "It should be noted that..."
- "It is worth mentioning..."
- "Both approaches have their merits" (when one is clearly better)
- "5 Things I Learned About X" (listicle structure)
- "TL;DR" sections
- Emoji in prose
- "Subscribe for more"
- "In conclusion..."
- Any marketing or product announcement language
